Building a Pizza Price Predictor

A pizzeria charges based on diameter. You collect data from their menu and want to predict the price of any size. Linear regression finds the best line — and the matrix form shows you exactly how.

What's happening: The design matrix X has a column of 1s (for the intercept B0) and a column of x-values (for the slope B1). The magic formula B = (X'X)-1X'Y gives us the exact same B0 and B1 we'd get from the formulas, but it generalizes beautifully to multiple predictors. In R: lm(Y ~ X, x=TRUE) stores the design matrix.

Scramble the Y — Kill the Signal

What happens if you randomly shuffle the response variable? The regression should find... nothing.
Load data above first, then scramble!
From the lecture: When Y is scrambled, the full model can't explain much more than the reduced model (just the mean). R² drops near zero, the F-statistic becomes tiny, and the p-value shoots up. The relationship was in the pairing of X and Y — destroy that and there's nothing left.

The F Distribution — A Ratio of Variances

Imagine two factories claiming equal quality control. You sample from each and compute the ratio of their variances. If they're truly equal, this ratio follows an F distribution. Extreme values mean one factory is much more variable than the other.

F = (χ²d1 / d1) / (χ²d2 / d2) — a ratio of two independent chi-square variables, each divided by their degrees of freedom.

Click Simulate to build the F distribution from chi-squares!
Building intuition: Each simulated F value is literally: generate d1 standard normals, square and sum them, divide by d1; then do the same with d2; take the ratio. The red curve is the theoretical F density. They should match perfectly — just like the lecture's R demo with rf() and df().

Critical Values — When Is the Ratio "Too Extreme"?

In ANOVA, we compute an F-statistic and ask: "Could this ratio have arisen by chance?" The right tail gives the p-value.

Does Practicing Free Throws Actually Help?

A basketball coach tracks how many free throws each player practices per week and their game accuracy. The ANOVA approach asks: does knowing practice hours (full model) predict accuracy significantly better than just guessing the average (reduced model)?

Full vs Reduced Model Comparison

Click Generate to see the ANOVA table
The ANOVA logic: Reduced model (B1 = 0) says practice doesn't matter — everyone's accuracy equals the grand mean. Full model (Y = B0 + B1*X) says practice helps. The F-statistic = [(reduced error - full error) / 1] / [full error / (n-2)]. A big F means the full model explains much more than the reduced. Set slope to 0 and see the F drop!

What R Shows You — summary() vs anova()

Connection: For simple regression (one predictor), the ANOVA F-statistic equals t² (the squared t-statistic from summary). They give exactly the same p-value! R's summary(lm) shows the t-test; anova(lm) shows the F-test. Same answer, different framing.

Do Dogs or Cats Sleep More? — t-test vs One-Way ANOVA

You track daily sleep hours for a sample of dogs and cats. You could use a t-test OR a one-way ANOVA — for two groups, they give exactly the same p-value! The ANOVA uses a design matrix with 0s and 1s to encode group membership.

Side-by-Side: t-test vs ANOVA

Generate data to compare results
They're the same test! For two groups with equal variance: F = t², and the p-values are identical. The ANOVA frames it as "does adding a group indicator reduce the error significantly?" while the t-test frames it as "is the difference in means significantly different from zero?" In R: t.test(a, b, var.equal=TRUE) equals anova(lm(y ~ group)). Use relevel() to change which group is "background" — for two groups it doesn't affect the p-value.
