A coffee machine should pour μ = 250 ml per cup with a known σ = 15 ml. You suspect it's overfilling. Measure some cups and test!

Adjust the sliders and hit Run!
Key idea: Z = (X̄ - μ₀) / (σ / √n). We convert to standard normal form by subtracting the mean and dividing by the standard error (σ/√n). A bigger sample size → smaller SE → easier to detect real differences.

📏Standard Error — Precision Grows With Sample Size

The standard error of the mean = σ / √n. Watch how the sampling distribution of the mean gets narrower as you increase n!

Why it matters: When n = 1, SE = σ (lots of uncertainty). By n = 100, SE = σ/10 (very precise). This is why the Z-test uses σ/√n — it accounts for how much averages vary, not individual observations.

📊Chi-Square Distribution — Sum of Squared Normals

Take k independent standard normal values, square each one, and sum them up. That sum follows a χ²(k) distribution. The mean = k.

Click Simulate to compare bootstrap vs. theory!
From the lecture: The red line is the theoretical χ²(k) density (dchisq in R). The blue histogram is the bootstrap — sum of k squared N(0,1) draws. They match beautifully!

🔔t-Distribution vs. Normal — Heavier Tails When df Is Small

When you estimate the standard deviation from your sample, the test statistic follows a t-distribution instead of a normal. At small df, the tails are fatter (more conservative). As df → ∞, t → Z.

From the lecture: At df = 5, you need to be further from the center to get the same p-value as with a normal. At df = 1000, they're essentially identical. This is why big sample sizes make the t-test and Z-test equivalent.

💥Why Z Breaks When You Estimate σ

The lecture's key "aha" moment: if you use sd(sample) instead of the true σ in a Z-test, the p-values are no longer uniform under H₀. Switch to the t-distribution and it's fixed!
