t-tests (equal/unequal/paired), Normality Testing, Wilcoxon, & Linear Regression

Equal Variance vs Welch's t-test

The standard t-test assumes equal variance. Welch's t-test does not — and has virtually no power penalty. As the lecture says: "There is no reason not to use it."

Click Compare to see both tests under H0 (same means, unequal variance)
Key insight: When variances are equal, both tests perform identically. When variances differ, the equal-variance t-test gives incorrect p-values (too many false positives), while Welch's remains correct. R defaults to var.equal=FALSE for this reason.

Paired t-test — The Power of Pairing

When each subject is measured before and after treatment, pairing removes between-subject variability. The test becomes: is the mean of the differences different from zero?

Click Simulate to compare paired vs unpaired power
Why pairing works: Subjects vary enormously (a mouse weighing 40g vs 12g), but each individual's change is consistent. By testing the differences, we remove the huge between-subject variability, leaving only the small within-subject noise. Try setting between-subject SD high and within-subject SD low to see the dramatic power gain.

Is Your Data Normal? — QQ Plots & the KS Test

The t-test assumes normality. We can check this visually with a QQ plot and formally with the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test.

Select a distribution and click Generate!
Reading QQ plots: If points fall on the diagonal line, the data is approximately normal. S-curves = heavy/light tails. Banana shapes = skewness. The KS test gives a formal p-value: low p = evidence against normality.

The Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test — No Normality Needed

Replace every value with its rank, turning an unknown distribution into a known one. Then ask: could we see this separation of ranks by chance?

Blue Whales vs Mice (from lecture)

Whales (kg): 108000, 104000, 102000 → Ranks: 4, 5, 6
Mice (kg): 0.0001, 0.0002, 0.0003 → Ranks: 1, 2, 3
C(6,3) = 20 possible rank assignments. Only 1 gives ranks {4,5,6} for whales. Two-sided p = 2/20 = 0.10
Click Simulate to compare t-test vs Wilcoxon power
Trade-off: The Wilcoxon test makes no distributional assumptions — great when normality fails. But it has less power than the t-test when data IS normal (especially at small n). For non-normal data, the Wilcoxon can actually be MORE powerful. Try the "Skewed" or "Heavy-tailed" distributions to see this.

What to look for: Left plot (Residuals vs Fitted): no pattern = good; funnel shape = unequal variance; curve = nonlinearity. Right plot (QQ of residuals): points on the line = normality assumption met.

ANOVA Decomposition — Where Does the Variance Go?
