Multiple Hypothesis Testing — Bonferroni, Benjamini-Hochberg FDR & When 20,000 Tests Go Wrong

P-Value Distribution Under the Null Hypothesis

You run an RNA-seq experiment with two conditions and test 20,000 genes. But suppose the truth is: no gene is actually different between conditions. Every gene is a null hypothesis. What happens to the p-values?
Click "Simulate Null Experiment" to generate p-values for 20,000 genes where NOTHING is different
Key insight: When the null hypothesis is true, p-values are uniformly distributed between 0 and 1. This means about 5% of genes will have p < 0.05 purely by chance. With 20,000 genes, that's roughly 1,000 false positives! This is the fundamental problem that motivates multiple testing correction. In R: runif(20000) gives you p-values that look just like this.

Bonferroni Correction — The Conservative Approach

To control the probability of making ANY false positive (family-wise error rate), simply divide your threshold by the number of tests. With 20,000 tests and α = 0.05: the Bonferroni threshold = 0.05 / 20,000 = 2.5 × 10-6.

Click "Run Experiment" to see Bonferroni in action
Bonferroni: Adjusted p-value = min(1, raw_p × N). Equivalently, use threshold α/N instead of α. This controls the family-wise error rate (FWER): the probability of making even ONE false positive is at most α. Very safe, but very conservative — at small sample sizes with modest effects, few genes may reach this extreme threshold. In R: p.adjust(p, method="bonferroni").

Benjamini-Hochberg FDR — A Less Conservative Approach

Instead of preventing ANY false positives, BH controls the expected proportion of false discoveries. If you set FDR = 0.10, on average 10% of the genes you call "significant" will be false positives — a much more practical standard for genomics.

The BH algorithm: (1) Rank all N p-values from smallest to largest. (2) For rank k, compute the BH line: k × FDR / N. (3) Going from the largest rank down, find the first p-value that falls below its BH line. (4) Declare that gene and all genes with smaller p-values as discoveries. At rank k=1 (the single best gene), the BH threshold = FDR/N = Bonferroni! But for higher ranks, BH is progressively more lenient. In R: p.adjust(p, method="BH").

Head-to-Head: No Correction vs Bonferroni vs BH FDR

Run the same experiment through all three approaches and see the tradeoff: more discoveries means more power, but also more false positives. Which method strikes the right balance?
Click "Compare Methods" to see the head-to-head results
The tradeoff: No correction catches the most true positives but is flooded with false positives. Bonferroni has almost zero false positives but misses many real signals (low power). BH FDR is the middle ground — it tolerates a controlled proportion of false discoveries in exchange for finding many more real signals. That's why genomics papers almost universally report FDR-adjusted p-values. Try setting effect size low and see how Bonferroni misses nearly everything!
